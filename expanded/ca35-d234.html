E3: The San Diego frontier war, 1839–1846 — a multi-year arc of uprising, alliance, and campaign

Scans: n73–n84 · n97–n120 · n149–n167 · n223 · n245–n252 · n428 | Orig pp. V·73–120, VI·27–167, VI·100, VI·122–129, VIII·64 | Type: running abstract of SD-district military/justice correspondence (Savage copy) | Docs: 234, 251, 253, 281, 309, 318, 328, 349, 350 (1839) · 433, 457 (1841) · 605, 665, 675, 683 (1842) · 871, 1101, 1149, 1150 (1845–46)

A decade-long thread that the volume lets one follow almost continuously:
- 1839 outbreak (Tomo V): the "indios sublevados" cluster — the siege of Mission Guadalupe, a feared attack on San Diego, and the apprehension of "el indio Alejo" (234/251/253); then the armed response — an LA relief expedition of 15–20 men (309) and foreigner+vecino militia mobilizations "con sus personas y armas" (318/328), standing down when Sta Bárbara troops arrive (349/350).
- 1841 (Tomo VI): the SD frontier war "in detail" (Aguilar's bundle, Doc 457) — the Graham-Affair circular published, E. Fink arming the pueblo + the Comandante de Frontera lending muskets, and a joint campaign against the Jamul (Jacum) Indians allied with named Indian captains + the Diegueños; Doc 433 has the San-Felipe gentile + Jamul killed at Mission Guadalupe, vecinos disarmed "excepto los 3 ingleses."
- 1842 (Tomo VI): juez José Joaquín Ortega gathers the gentile captains at San José del Valle, who pledge peace and to prevent robberies "otros de su clase" (605); then Antonio Mª Lugo's Sierra party reports the Chaguanosos "con 4 o más cristianos" behind the Sierra de San Fernando (665); the Jamul/Jacum uprising scare at SD (only 5 men, 675) — resolved when the Jacum captains appear, deny any uprising, name the "Baluartes" murderers, and peace is reached "via Tijuana y Jacume" (683).
- 1845–46 (Tomo VIII): the war never really ends — a 20-gentile attack on Rancho San Francisco repelled (1101); a March-1846 campaign in which Vicente Lugo's party finds "60, bien armados" Indians at the río de las Ánimas and asks reinforcement to attack (1149/1150).

Significance: the single richest continuous record in the volume of the San Diego / trans-frontier Indian war as the californios actually experienced it — not one "revolt" but a decade of raiding, punitive campaigning, indigenous diplomacy (peace-pledges by named gentile captains), and shifting alliances (Diegueños and some captains as allies against the Jamul; apostate ex-neophyte "cristianos" among the raiders). Central to [[indian-raiding-article]] and to [[californio-article]]'s frontier-defense/self-rule argument; the peace-brokering documents complicate any purely-military reading. Pairs with C-A 34's frontier material. ⚠ Names ("Pájaro," "Jacum/Jamul," "Baluartes," the captain names) are scale-2 and need scale-4 verification; cross-check the campaign sequence against Bancroft vol.
