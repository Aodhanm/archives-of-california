E2: The "Pájaro" affair — Sonora's cross-border claim to name a Gentile *Capitán General de San Felipe* (Oct 1839)

Scans: n94–n96 | Orig pp. ~V·94–96 | Type: abstract of an inter-jurisdictional correspondence (Savage copy) | Docs: 285 (+ the 1841 sequel at 433)

A borderlands jurisdiction dispute over indigenous authority at the Colorado-river crossing. Sonoran officials moved to **name a Gentile Indian Capitán General de San Felipe — a recognized indigenous headman-office astride the Sonora↔California overland route — and the San Diego authorities rejected it, treating the appointment as a Sonoran intrusion into Alta-California's jurisdiction over the frontier gentiles (Doc 285, spanning n94–96). The same San-Felipe gentile leadership ("Pájaro"/"A. Chico"[?]) reappears in 1841 (Doc 433) allied with the Jamul Indians who "rose up" and were killed at Mission Guadalupe, when the SD vecinos were disarmed "excepto los 3 ingleses."

Significance: a rare, concrete instance of two Mexican departments contesting the right to constitute indigenous authority on the shared desert frontier — indigenous office as an object of inter-state rivalry, and the Colorado crossing as a jurisdictional seam.
