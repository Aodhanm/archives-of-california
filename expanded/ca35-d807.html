E8: ⭐ The Jones–Micheltorena Convention — settling Commodore Thomas ap Catesby Jones's seizure of Monterey (Los Angeles, 20 Jan 1843)

Scans: n296–n299 | Orig pp. VII·38–41 (doc pp.230–233) | Type: near-verbatim abstract of a bando reproducing the convention's articles + the Commodore's reply (Savage copy) | Docs: 807, 808

The volume's keystone document. On 19 Oct 1842 Commodore Thomas ap Catesby Jones seized Monterey in the mistaken belief that the U.S. and Mexico were at war, then — undeceived — restored the Mexican flag. The diplomatic settlement moved to Los Angeles ("por ser ahí residencia del gobierno"), where Gov. Manuel Micheltorena and Jones agreed an 8-article convention, published as a prefectural bando (Angeles, 20 Jan 1843; publicized 24–29 Jan):
1. the indemnity for "el ultraje hecho al pabellón mejicano" reserved to the two national governments;
2. private claims likewise reserved;
3. a mutual pledge that neither nation's forces "obre jamás contra las personas, buques, propiedades o personas de la otra nación";
4. the Monterey Capitulation signed by Jones and interim-governor J.B. Alvarado declared "nula y sin efecto";
5. the U.S. warships & merchantmen then at San Pedro to salute the Mexican flag within ten days;
6. Jones to hand over 500 cloth infantry uniforms in replacement of what the troops consumed on the forced march "al ocurrir a reparar el pueblo indefenso";
7. Jones to pay $1,500 spent on the general alarm and replace a ruined set of military-band instruments;
8. the convention drawn up quadruplicate in English & Spanish.
A following narrative (Doc 808) records Jones's own account — that he "había ocupado Monterrey en el concepto que había guerra con los E.U., pero, desengañado, restableció la bandera mejicana." (The spiking of San Diego's 8 fort cannons by the crew of the American ship "Alert," Doc 780, belongs to the same war-scare.)

Significance: a verbatim californio-side text of the Jones affair's diplomatic settlement — the single most historically significant document in the volume and one likely uncited by Bancroft from this Savage copy. It documents Los Angeles serving as the seat of government for a formal Mexican–U.S. negotiation, the explicit annulment of the Alvarado capitulation, and the material reparations (uniforms, $1,500, band instruments).
