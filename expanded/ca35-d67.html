E1: Hartnell's 1839 mission-visita and the labor-collapse of the ex-missions — the flight of the San Luis Rey Indians (June 1839)

Scans: n21 · n26–n27 · n35 · n47–n48 | Orig pp. 21–48 (doc pp.158-9, 198-9, 233-4, 349-54) | Type: near-verbatim abstract with a quoted enclosure + a census (Savage copy) | Docs: 67, 82, 106, 148, 150

Three linked June-1839 documents in which Visitador General de Misiones William E. P. Hartnell works through the LA prefecture to stem the collapse of the secularized southern missions:
- Doc 67 (14 June): the Gov orders the LA prefecture to aid Hartnell's reorganization of the missions by returning the dispersed indígenas to the establishments, "obrando prudentemente" toward some indigenous families who by service, age or circumstance merit being separated from the communities.
- Doc 82 (14 June) — the centerpiece: Hartnell forwards the San Luis Rey administrator's letter of 12 June, quoted at length. The administrator reports sending the Indian alcaldes Samuel and Pablo out "a recoger los indios que andan fuera de las Misiones," warns that "se han huido una porción de ellos y como cada día sigue esta falta aumentándose, los trabajos y el establecimiento van caminando a su ruina," and threatens "hacer mi renuncia, porque conozco que no se encuentra apoyo para el arreglo de los indígenas." He complains that the fugitive Indians throng the LA pueblos and, "en los días festivos," commit "bastantes escándalos." Hartnell asks the LA authorities to place all the fugitive San Luis Rey Indians found in those environs at the disposal of the comisionados, and to enjoin them against future desertions.
- Doc 106 (14 June): Hartnell restates it flatly — the fugitive Indians must be made to return to the establishments "para que estos se salven de la ruina que les amenaza por la falta de trabajadores."
- Docs 148 & 150 (10 July) — the hard number: M. Domínguez sends a list of the neophytes of Mission San Luis Rey, and a census of "los Indios de la Misión de S. Luis Rey actualmente residentes en ella" gives Hombres 29, Mujeres 11, Total 40 indios. After decades as one of the largest missions in the province (thousands of neophytes at its peak), San Luis Rey is reduced to 40 resident Indians by mid-1839 — a stark quantitative measure of exactly the collapse Hartnell and the administrator describe.
- Doc 168 (8 July) — where they went: Administrator Valenzuela of La Purísima sends a list of that mission's "neófitos desertores" with their whereabouts — 22 men + 7 women in Los Tulares, 10 men at Monterey, the rest at Los Angeles & Santa Bárbara (45 total). This is the crucial link: the largest share of the fugitives went to Los Tulares — the interior San Joaquín raiding zone — tying the mission-labor collapse directly to the interior horse-raiding economy (the ex-neophytes who joined/guided the raids). Cf. C-A 34's fugitive-neophyte material and the [[indian-raiding-article]] 1838–40 crisis.

Significance: a sharp, datable primary window on the labor economics of secularization's failure in the south — the ex-mission establishments could not function once the Indian workforce dispersed, and the response of the Visitador and the californio district authorities was a coordinated round-up to compel the fugitives back to mission labor. It is doubly valuable: (a) it shows secularization producing not indigenous emancipation but a labor crisis met with coercion (the alcaldes-Samuel-and-Pablo round-ups, the "arreglo de los indígenas"); and (b) it captures indigenous agency by flight — mass desertion as the Indians' own answer to the post-secularization regime, and its power to threaten the whole establishment "con su ruina." → [[californio-article]] (secularization, mission-land/labor consolidation, the Hartnell visita), [[indian-raiding-article]]/[[indian-affairs]] (mission-Indian flight & compelled return; pairs with the C-A 34 unfree-labor cluster E22 and the C-A 34 Hartnell/San-Luis-Rey material).
