E6: The New Mexican caravans and the raiding economy, 1839–1848 — trade, herd-inspection, and the Chaguanosos

Scans: n107 · n115 · n197–n208 · n221–n239 · n312 · n441 | Orig pp. V·107/115, VI·197–239, VII·54, VIII·77 | Type: running abstract of passports, convoy-control instructions & herd-inspection reports (Savage copy) | Docs: 325, 343 (1839) · 544, 549, 552 (1841) · 606, 617–624, 644 (1842) · 845 (1843) · 1181 (1848). Ties C-A 34 E28.

The annual Santa Fe / Abiquiú → Los Angeles caravan and its shadow, the horse-raiding economy, traced year by year:
- 1839: arrivals of 75 New Mexicans (325) and Salazar's 75-trader caravan (343).
- 1841: the Esteban Vigil caravan with a passport + 9-article convoy-control order including a herd-verification/anti-rustling protocol on the return (544); the Chaguanosos — Argüello's measures to "evitar el robo de los Chaguanoses" (549) and 2 Indians scouting them (552).
- 1842 — the enforcement in action (Rowland–Vigil): Bandini claims horses from "compradores de Nuevo Méjico" and Argüello orders the caravan's caballada inspected (606); written instructions + passports/escort-partidas for Vigil & Rowland (617–621); the Juez de Campo A.I. Ávila posts 20 men under José Palomares to watch the departure — Rowland leaves with 800 animals, ~100 head suspiciously branded, and a party of 8+11 is sent "sobre el enemigo" (624); the tally — "salieron el 18 de La Puerta del Cajón 144 N. Mejicanos con 1,150 bestias legales, dejando 9 animales por carecer de la venta" (644).
- 1843: the return caravan — "a list of 21 individuals returning to New Mexico with 232 animals" (845).
- 1848 (under US rule): a New Mexican caravan gathering at San Bernardino, its commanders asking a colonel for munitions "pues no las pueden conseguir en las tiendas" (1181).

Significance: an unusually granular, quantified record of the New Mexican trade and the californio state's attempt to police it — passports, convoy commanders, and above all the herd-inspection régime designed to separate legally-purchased horses from stolen ones as the caravans left through the Cajón. It documents both the licit sarape-for-horses trade and its inseparability from the interior raiding economy (the Chaguanosos; apostate "cristianos"), and shows the apparatus persisting into the American period. Core to [[indian-raiding-article]] and to [[californio-article]] (frontier governance / the Old Spanish Trail terminus); dovetails with C-A 34 E28. ⚠ The tallies (144 men / 1,150 beasts / 9 rejected; 21 men / 232 animals) and "Rowland"/"Vigil"/"Chaguanoses" are scale-2 readings and must be verified at scale 4; cross-check against Hafen's Old Spanish Trail literature and Bancroft before quoting the numbers.
