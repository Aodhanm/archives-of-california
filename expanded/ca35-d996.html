E9: The 1844 smallpox epidemic and the Los Angeles "Bando de Salubridad"

Scans: n378–n379 · n386–n387 · n390 | Orig pp. VIII·14–15, VIII·21–23, VIII·26 | Type: abstracts of justice-encargado reports + a full public-health bando (Savage copy) | Docs: 996, 997, 1007, 1015, 1017, 1024

The 1844 smallpox scare and the municipal response. The disease thread runs through Hugo Reid's San Gabriel reports — first 3 patients he believes are the milder "variola vacuna" (997), then reclassified as "no de viruelas… sino de una sarna muy fea" (1007) — while Santa Bárbara requests cowpox vaccine ("pus vacuno") as cases mount (1015) and later sends it "extraído de unos niños de buena salud" in three glass vials (1024). It culminates in the Ayuntamiento's 11-article "Bando de Salubridad" (Doc 1017, 4 July 1844): police celadores to keep drinking water clean; slaughter-meat to be healthy, kept extramuros and stored ventilated; every ship at San Pedro to await a Junta de Sanidad inspection, with quarantine if infected; no contagious persons admitted; frequent bathing and clean houses; dietary rules (no chile/stimulants, wash salted meat); sulfur fumigation, or daily vinegar; the bando read house-to-house by a celador.

Significance: an unusually complete frontier public-health document — a small pueblo's articulated theory of contagion and prevention (water, meat, ventilation, maritime quarantine, fumigation, diet) issued by its own restored ayuntamiento, tied to a datable epidemic and to a working vaccination supply chain (arm-to-arm cowpox from healthy children).
